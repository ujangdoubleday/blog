There's a specific kind of feeling that doesn't have a great English word for it. That moment when you return to a place from your past and feel a ghost version of yourself standing right next to you. _End of Beginning_ by Djo is exactly that feeling — bottled, compressed into four minutes, and unleashed directly into your chest.

And the wild part? Almost nobody heard it when it first came out.

## The Slow Burn That Became a Wildfire

_End of Beginning_ was quietly dropped in September 2022 as the sixth track on Djo's second album, [_Decide_](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decide_(album)>). No massive promo campaign. No viral moment. Just... there. Sitting on Spotify like a message in a bottle, waiting.

Then 2024 happened.

TikTok users started posting montages of their hometowns — the Chicago River shimmering under a bridge, the L train rattling through the Loop, old dorm hallways, college quads — all soundtracked by that opening synth chord. One after another after another. The algorithm ate it up. And suddenly, a song that debuted with barely a ripple [hit the US Billboard Hot 100 at number 51 with 8.4 million streams in a single week](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End_of_Beginning).

But here's what really broke the chart: in January 2026, when Stranger Things finally ended, fans flooded back to the song and it [topped the UK Singles Chart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End_of_Beginning) — becoming the second Stranger Things-associated song to reach number one in Britain after Kate Bush's "Running Up That Hill" in 2022. Iconic company.

## So... Who Even Is Djo?

This is where it gets interesting. Djo is Joe Keery — yes, _Steve Harrington_ from Stranger Things. [He was born on April 25, 1992, in Newburyport, Massachusetts, and moved to Chicago to attend The Theatre School at DePaul University, graduating in 2014 with a BFA in acting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Keery).

But acting wasn't the whole story. While auditioning hundreds of times in Chicago (literally — after graduating from DePaul, Keery went to over a hundred auditions), he was also deep in the city's DIY music scene. He cut his teeth with the band [Post Animal](https://screenrant.com/joe-keery-band-music-solo-artist-djo/), a psychedelic rock outfit described by the Chicago Tribune as "if Tame Impala listened to a lot of Black Sabbath and were signed to Elephant 6." Yeah, _that_ kind of band.

Then 2016 hit and Stranger Things happened. Fame arrived fast. In 2019, Keery made the decision to leave the band and close friends and take his Chicago memories to Los Angeles. He never stopped making music though — he just did it under the stage name Djo (pronounced "Joe," which, yes, is just his name — the man contains multitudes).

What's fascinating is the deliberate separation of his two identities. In [an interview with NME](https://www.nme.com/news/music/joe-keery-releases-new-single-change-ahead-of-sophomore-solo-album-decide-3253939), Keery said he adopted the Djo stage name specifically to separate his music from Steve Harrington — but then ended up liking "the camaraderie that it creates." As Djo, he becomes more introspective and experimental — a separation that allows him to explore music authentically without being confined by expectations tied to his acting career.

## Chicago As a Character, Not Just a Setting

Before you can understand this song, you have to understand what Chicago means to Joe Keery. This isn't just geography. This is identity.

In a [feature interview with NME](https://www.nme.com/features/djo-on-his-tiktok-smash-end-of-beginning-3601275), Keery described the song as "a reflection on my time in my early twenties living in Chicago, where I went to college and graduated and played in a few bands" — capturing that anxious feeling of being so focused on the future that you don't realize what a special time you're living through.

In the same NME interview, he described the Chicago scene with genuine warmth: "I was just a fanboy for a long time. Everybody was really hustling and to see people being so creative and inventive was really inspiring to me with my own music." That's the environment the song is trying to capture — not just a city, but a feeling of being surrounded by people who were all reaching for something.

That's not poetic license. That's actual memory. That's what makes this song work — it's _so_ personal that somehow it became universal.

## Lyrical Breakdown: Saying Goodbye to Who You Were

### The Chorus That Broke TikTok

Let's be honest, this is the part that got everyone:

> "And when I'm back in Chicago, I feel it / Another version of me, I was in it / I wave goodbye to the end of beginning"

These lines describe the "ghosts" of our past selves that we encounter when we revisit old haunts — he can see the person he used to be, but he is no longer "in" that life. [SORI Magazine's analysis](https://soriplay.com/interpretations/en/end%20of%20beginning::djo) puts it well: even though he moved to Los Angeles for acting, the values from his Chicago era remain his core.

The phrase "end of beginning" itself is doing something clever. It's not the _beginning_ of an end (that sounds sad and final). It's the _end_ of a beginning — acknowledging that a foundational, formative chapter has closed. There's grief in that, but also pride. You got through the beginning. Now what?

And that's exactly why it went so viral. Everyone has a Chicago. Everyone has a place, a time, a version of themselves they can only visit now — never inhabit again.

### "Remember Twenty-Four?"

In the song's first verse, Djo speaks of tears falling for the end of an era, and the need to save the last cry for when it is truly needed. Then he asks himself, "Remember twenty-four?" — [a reference to 2016, the year Stranger Things arrived on Netflix](https://auralcrave.com/en/2026/01/08/the-meaning-of-djos-end-of-beginning-joe-keerys-goodbye-to-steve-harrington/), when Keery was 24 years old.

So he's literally timestamping his own life. Age 24, the year everything changed. That specificity is what separates good songwriting from great songwriting. He's not talking about "youth" as an abstract concept — he's talking about a specific moment in 2016 when his phone rang and nothing was ever the same.

### "You Take the Man Out of the City, Not the City Out the Man"

This line is technically a play on the old idiom, but Keery flips the priority. Even though he moved to Los Angeles for his acting career, the values and experiences from his Chicago era remain his core. [As analyzed on SORI](https://soriplay.com/interpretations/en/end%20of%20beginning::djo), it's a quiet assertion of identity against the pull of Hollywood.

The implication is almost defiant: you can put me in LA, put me on red carpets, put me in interviews — but something essential about who I became in those basement gigs and cheap apartments on the north side? That doesn't leave.

### "Trouble Getting Used to the Self-Indulgence"

This one hits different if you know the context. [According to SORI's interpretation](https://soriplay.com/interpretations/en/end%20of%20beginning::djo), this line likely refers to his transition into Hollywood and the "fame" lifestyle — highlighting the friction between his grounded past and his current reality as a public figure.

There's something genuinely uncomfortable about someone famous enough to be recognized on the street admitting that fame feels weird. That the money and access feels excessive. That part of him misses being broke and unknown and just making music in a Chicago basement with his friends.

### "Goodbye, Farewell / Mistook It for Hell"

The hindsight gut-punch. At the time, he may have struggled or felt stuck — but [with hindsight, he realizes it was a beautiful, necessary beginning](https://soriplay.com/interpretations/en/end%20of%20beginning::djo).

How many of us are currently living through something we'll only appreciate in retrospect? That's the uncomfortable question this lyric asks. The thing you're complaining about right now? Someday you might wave goodbye to it with tears in your eyes.

## The Sound: Why It Hits So Hard

Musically, [_Decide_ is classified as a synth-pop, synthwave, and alt-pop album](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decide_(album)>), representing a deliberate departure from the psychedelic rock of his debut. The album was written and produced alongside collaborator Adam Thein during the COVID-19 lockdowns, and recorded at The Sound Factory in Los Angeles.

The production does something sneaky. It features heavy use of analog synthesizers, mimicking the sound of late 2010s dream pop — which means the instrumental palette itself feels like a memory. Like something you've heard before but can't quite place. Your brain keeps trying to file it under "the past" even when it's playing in the present.

The dynamic structure is also doing a lot of emotional work. The verse is hushed, almost hesitant — like someone gathering courage to say something hard. Then the chorus opens up with that synth wash and Keery's voice rises, and suddenly you're not in a bedroom anymore. You're in the middle of a feeling that's too big for a room.

[The Young Folks' review of _Decide_](https://www.theyoungfolks.com/review/167000/djo-decide-album-review/) nailed it: "End of Beginning" is the best utilization of Keery's vocals — singing with a "punctuated sense of vulnerability," it's one of the truly laid-bare moments on the album. The album as a whole lets instrumentation and lyrics align seamlessly, and "End of Beginning" is where that alignment hits hardest.

## The TikTok Phenomenon and Why It Made Sense

The specific way this song went viral is worth thinking about. It wasn't memes. It wasn't a dance. It was people filming their hometowns.

[As documented by the Official Charts](https://www.officialcharts.com/chart-news/djo-end-of-beginning-joe-keery-trend/), TikTok users flooded feeds with travel videos of their cities — the Chicago River, the L train, fireworks over the lake, Montrose Beach. Other videos strung together footage from Chicago-based shows like _The Bear_ and _Shameless_. All soundtracked by the same opening chord.

[SORI Magazine's cultural analysis](https://soriplay.com/interpretations/en/end%20of%20beginning::djo) summarizes it well: it became the definitive anthem for "nostalgia" trends, resonating globally because it perfectly captures the universal feeling of a coming-of-age story ending.

There's something profound about that. In a media landscape built for instant gratification, a quiet song about growing up and letting go became the thing that broke through. Because apparently, underneath all the content and scroll and noise, people are just quietly grieving their own past selves. The song gave them a container for that grief.

## The Stranger Things Dimension

When the Stranger Things finale aired on December 31, 2025, fans did something spontaneous and beautiful: they declared _End of Beginning_ the unofficial farewell anthem for the show. Not because it was on the soundtrack — [there is actually no trace of Djo's song in the show's official soundtrack](https://auralcrave.com/en/2026/01/08/the-meaning-of-djos-end-of-beginning-joe-keerys-goodbye-to-steve-harrington/).

They chose it because of what the song _is_. Keery spent nearly a decade playing Steve Harrington — a character who grew from a high school bully into one of the most beloved figures in the show. And here was Keery, writing about saying goodbye to a version of himself, about waving farewell to a chapter that shaped him.

[According to Just Jared](https://www.justjared.com/2026/01/13/end-of-beginning-lyrics-meaning-djo-aka-stranger-things-joe-keery-becomes-a-huge-hit-years-later/), Keery himself said the song "mirrors the final season of Stranger Things" — about what it means to grow up and look back at a section of your life and kind of yearn for that, but then also to have a deep appreciation for what happened.

Intentional or not? Either way: perfect.

## What Djo Said About All This

In his own words, this is what _End of Beginning_ means to him:

> _"It's about saying goodbye to a certain part of your life, and it being a sad thing, but also looking onward."_ — [Joe Keery via AWAL](https://x.com/AWAL/status/1777805129884025260)

And from a [Variety interview](https://variety.com/2024/music/global/joe-keery-djo-end-of-beginning-tiktok-stranger-things-final-season-1235945593/): "It's about longing for something in the past and then also trying to appreciate the future and the present moment that you're in."

Both sad and hopeful. Neither victorious nor defeated. Just... honest. That's genuinely hard to pull off in a pop song, and Keery makes it sound effortless.

## The Deeper Fear the Song Doesn't Name

Here's a reading of the song that's darker than the surface suggests, and I can't stop thinking about it.

[Songtell's analysis](https://songtell.com/djo/end-of-beginning) argues that perhaps this song is not about outgrowing a city at all, but about the terrifying possibility that we never actually change. The repetition of the Chicago lines suggests that no matter how much time passes, the "version" of him that lived there is always waiting in the wings — less about moving forward and more about acknowledging that we are always carrying our past selves like heavy luggage.

He uses repetition like a mantra to convince himself he has moved on. But the song is cyclical. The nostalgia doesn't end. You just learn to carry it differently.

Which is maybe the most honest thing anyone's said about growing up.

## Quick Djo Discography Guide (If You're New Here)

| ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---- | ----------------------------------------- |
| [_Twenty Twenty_](https://www.nme.com/reviews/djo-twenty-twenty-review-joe-keery-2547210) | 2019 | Psychedelic rock, very Post Animal energy |

| [_The Crux_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Keery)                                     | 2025 | Indie rock, more mature and expansive     |

NME gave _Twenty Twenty_ a glowing four-star review, describing Keery as ["a musician of very high calibre"](https://www.nme.com/reviews/djo-twenty-twenty-review-joe-keery-2547210) who dabbles in inventive, warped psychedelia — comparing him to Tame Impala and Ariel Pink. That was 2019. Nobody was ready.

If _End of Beginning_ is your entry point — and for most people in 2024-2026, it was — start with _Decide_ in full. Then go backwards to _Twenty Twenty_ for the weirder, rawer version of this guy. Then _The Crux_ for where he ended up.

You'll find the throughline: someone constantly processing who he was versus who he's becoming, and making genuinely great music out of the tension.

## Conclusion

_End of Beginning_ is special for a reason that's hard to articulate but easy to feel: it understands that nostalgia isn't just about missing the past. It's about the strange, disorienting experience of realizing you've already lived through something that mattered. That the significant chapter — the one that shaped everything — is already done.

The beginning has ended. Now what?

Keery doesn't answer that question. He just stands at that threshold with you, synths swelling, voice cracking slightly, and says: yeah. I felt that too.

And somehow that's enough.

Now if you'll excuse me, I need to drive around aimlessly at 1 AM with this on repeat and think about every city I've ever said goodbye to.
